The Work: The Joon Project

An experiment in community, built with bare hands and a lot of heart. This is the story of Phase 2 (2019-2023).

The Genesis: A Seed Planted in 2015

The idea for what would become The Joon Project began as conversations among friends in 2015. We were a diverse group—geologists, social engineers, computer whizzes—united by a shared discontent with the failing systems around us. We dreamed of a place that could harmonize humanity, nature, and technology. It was a vision born from my personal journey, polished through travels and countless discussions.

2020: Building in the Stillness of a Frozen World

When the COVID-19 pandemic brought the world to a halt, it became the catalyst for action. The global crisis proved how desperately we needed new, resilient ways to live. On a piece of family land far from the city—a place with no roads, no electricity, just an old hut and a spring—we began. We didn't have much, just donated tools and a powerful dream. We decided to build.

From Bare Hands to a Home

Our first steps were foundational. We installed a water pump, a challenging task that taught us about perseverance and teamwork. We gathered wood, mud, and straw to build our first small hut. This humble structure became everything: our kitchen, our office, our shelter. It was a symbol of what community spirit could achieve—built not with money, but with passion, vision, and hope.

The Rhythm of Community: Music, Art, and Connection

The Joon Project was never just about construction; it was about creating a culture. We started free, live music sessions every Friday, bringing together local artists and youth. We organized the "Joon Camp Festival," a celebration of creativity with free bamboo tattoos, wood carving, and painting, all operating without a single rupee exchanged. It was a revolution of hearts, proving that connection is the most valuable currency.

The Heart of the Work: The Orphanage

A core part of our mission became our collaboration with a local orphanage. We stepped in not as donors, but as family. We introduced yoga and meditation to help the children heal unseen wounds. We implemented a system of shared roles—finance, kitchen, stationery—giving them responsibility and ownership. We showed them that education isn't confined to a classroom; it's in the garden, the workshop, and the community.

Lessons in Impermanence: The Halt

Our journey was not without hardship. A wild storm blew our volunteer tent apart, testing our resolve. A devastating flood washed away thousands of rupees' worth of wood we had prepared for a new volunteer house. Finally, a life-altering accident involving a co-founder's father forced us to halt all operations. It was a painful reminder that life is unpredictable and that our first duty is to care for our own.

Conclusion: The Legacy of Joon

The Joon Project, as a physical entity, came to a pause in 2023. But it was never a failure. It was a necessary and beautiful experiment. It taught us how to build, how to lead, how to fail, and how to get back up. The lessons, the connections, and the unshakeable proof that a better way is possible became the fertile soil from which our next phase, The HUNATE Movement, would grow.

A citizen-led movement to restore balance between Humanity, Nature, and Technology.
