The Problems We See: A Failing System

At this time, things are going very bad. We, who call ourselves smart, are not acting smart anymore. Our human-made systems—in health, education, economics, and governance—are broken, leading to disconnection and destruction. This is a call for us to notice what is happening and start reflecting on our own lives.

I. The Human Condition

Our current systems are harming us at every level. We see a rise in depression, anxiety, and suicide. We eat lifeless food from factories and depend on industries that profit from our sickness. Our families are fractured, our communities are rare, and loneliness is an epidemic. We are addicted to consumerism and media, chasing fake desires instead of true meaning, leading to a deep inner emptiness.

II. Societal Structures

Our world is rife with division—by country, religion, race, and class, preventing us from seeing each other as one family. Our profit-based economy is the root cause of corruption, inequality, and the exploitation of both labor and nature. Our leaders often prioritize short-term power over long-term well-being, and our legal systems protect the powerful rather than delivering true justice. Our education system trains children to be obedient workers for this failing system, not awakened, critical thinkers.

III. Nature & Our Planet

We are deeply harming the Earth. We see melting glaciers, unpredictable seasons, and collapsing ecosystems. We treat land and water as commodities to be owned and sold, leading to massive deforestation, soil erosion, and pollution. Our industrial farming practices poison the soil, and seed monopolies threaten our food sovereignty. The mass slaughter of animals and disappearance of essential pollinators reveal a profound disconnection from the web of life.

IV. Technology & Our Tools

The very tools we created to help us are now causing more problems. If technology can create abundance, why do we still face hunger and scarcity? Our industries are built on polluting, extractive energy systems. The internet, designed to connect us, often leaves us more divided and addicted through manipulative algorithms. We are creating mountains of unrecyclable plastic waste, poisoning our planet and our bodies.

Our History

The Journey of Self-Discovery

My life has been a journey of questioning and seeking truth amidst these failing systems. Born in a remote village, my path led me through the confines of hostel life, the chaos of a nation in civil war, and personal struggles of addiction and loss. This period of being lost was also a period of awakening. It pushed me to travel, to learn from the road, and to seek wisdom in ancient practices. It was through this process of deep self-reflection that the seeds of the Hunate Movement were sown.

Read My Full Story

I was born into a family of rebels, the union of a Brahmin mother and a Newar father, both communist revolutionaries in a time when inter-caste marriage was forbidden. My life began in an animal shelter in Bhojpur, a place without roads or electricity, and was immediately shaped by turmoil. A family betrayal led to my mother leaving for the city of Damak with my newborn brother, while I was left behind with my grandmother. My earliest memories are of this separation, of navigating a world of adults alone, culminating in a terrifying solo journey from a dark room towards a distant celebration—a journey from darkness to light that still shapes me.

My childhood was a series of jarring transitions. I was moved to Damak, a modern city that was a world away from my village, and soon after sent to a hostel. Hostel life was a brutal education in survival. By day, I was a good student and athlete, but by night, I faced a world of bullying, abuse, and exploitation. I learned to be a fighter, a protector for my younger brother, and a chameleon who adapted to a harsh environment. It was here, surrounded by the children of wealthy families, that I first felt the sting of inequality and began to question the systems that create such divides.

The Maoist Civil War intensified these feelings. My parents were jailed for their revolutionary activities, our family was torn apart, and a beloved cousin was killed. I witnessed violence firsthand—a shooting in front of our shop, a near-death bus accident, and the constant threat of conflict. These experiences, combined with a feeling of being an outcast at school, pushed me further to the fringes. After leaving the hostel, I fell into a cycle of drug addiction, theft, and aimlessness. My grades plummeted, and I was eventually suspended from school.

The turning point came after a friend died of a drug overdose, an event that landed me and my friends in jail. Facing the profound disappointment of my parents and the stark reality of my path, I knew I had to change. This began my conscious "Journey of Self-Discovery". I immersed myself in Vipassana meditation, which provided a path to heal from years of trauma. I started traveling, not as a tourist, but as a seeker. I worked on trucks, hitchhiked across Nepal and India, and volunteered at an alternative school, Maya Universe Academy, where I finally found a community that valued hands-on learning and global connection. It was during this period, from roughly 2013 onwards, that I synthesized my life experiences with conspiracy theories, alternative economics (like The Venus Project), and ancient wisdom to form the foundation of "The Joon Project," the precursor to HUNATE. This project was my answer to the question that had haunted me my whole life: in a world of failing systems, how can we build a new way to live? The journey of pain had finally transformed into a journey of purpose.

Our Core Mandate

Our Mission

To explore, understand, and reveal new systems of living that fundamentally heal the disconnections between humans and their true nature; between nature and its sacred place in our lives; and between technology and its purpose
