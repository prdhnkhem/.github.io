The Foundation:

A History of The Joon Project

Before Hunate, there was The Joon Project. This is the story of the hands-on fieldwork that moved beyond charity to uncover the deeper need for systemic change.

The Spark of an Idea

The Joon Project was not born in a boardroom; it was born from a simple, human connection. After years of personal transformation and travel, my path led me to a local orphanage. I didn't arrive as a savior or a donor with a grand plan. I came with a quiet curiosity, simply to observe and understand. What I saw was a system that, despite its good intentions, was failing the very children it was meant to protect.

The orphanage was clean, the children were fed, but they were not thriving. They were passive recipients of aid, lacking a sense of agency, purpose, or true family. The model was one of dependence. Donors would come, give money or goods, take photos, and leave. The children learned to be grateful, but they were not learning to be capable. I saw a cycle of dependency being created, a system that managed problems without ever solving them. It was in witnessing this that the core question of the Joon Project arose: What if we stopped treating these children as victims to be pitied and started treating them as human beings with immense potential to be nurtured?

From Charity to Community

Our approach was radically different. We didn't want to just give; we wanted to build. The first step was to shift the mindset from charity to community. We stopped being "visitors" and started being family. We ate with the children, played with them, and listened to their stories. We introduced daily practices of yoga, meditation, and art‚Äînot as hobbies, but as tools for self-regulation and self-expression.

"The most profound change occurred when we gave the children responsibility. We taught them to manage their own home. They created their own schedules, managed their own kitchen budget, and took ownership of their space. For the first time, they weren't just living in a building; they were creating a home."

We organized festivals and events without a budget, relying on creativity and collaboration. We built shelters from bamboo sourced locally. We proved, on a small scale, that a community could thrive on contribution, shared purpose, and resourcefulness rather than on a constant influx of external funding. The children began to transform. They became more confident, more responsible, and more joyful. They were no longer just orphans; they were brothers and sisters in a functioning, self-reliant family.

The Revelation: Treating Symptoms vs. Curing the Disease

The success of our work at the orphanage was deeply rewarding, but it also led to a sobering realization. We had created a beautiful, thriving island in the middle of a broken system. While we had changed the lives of a few dozen children, the fundamental problems in the wider world remained untouched. The very existence of orphanages was a symptom of a deeper societal disease: broken families, economic desperation, and a lack of community support.

We realized that our work, as impactful as it was, was still a patch. True, lasting change would not come from building better orphanages. It would come from creating a world where orphanages were no longer necessary. It required going to the root of the problem.

This was the critical lesson of The Joon Project. It was the fertile ground from which a much larger vision began to grow. The hands-on, practical experience proved that a holistic, self-sufficient model could work. Now, the challenge was to scale that model‚Äîto take the principles we had tested in a single home and apply them to the design of entire communities, and ultimately, to a new way of living for everyone.

From a Project to a Philosophy

The Joon Project was the laboratory. The Hunate Project is the resulting theory. It takes the lessons learned from this foundational work and expands them into a comprehensive blueprint for a new world. The journey continues from the practical to the philosophical.
