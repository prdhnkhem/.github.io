Khem's Story

Every great movement begins with a personal story. This is the journey of an individual seeking truth, a path of deconstruction and discovery that laid the essential groundwork for the Hunate philosophy.

FORMATIVE YEARS

My parents came from different worlds in Bhojpur—my father from a Pradhan, Newar family and my mother from a Parajuli, Brahmin family. In a village without roads or electricity, their paths crossed through politics, both rivals in the communist party. Their love story was one of rebellion and consequence; an inter-caste marriage led to their exile from their families and villages. They fled to Damak, Jhapa, with nothing, starting a new life where I was born in 1991. Life was a struggle. My father worked tirelessly—from a hotel waiter to a small shop owner—while my mother cared for us. We moved from one rented room to another, facing constant financial hardship. Yet, amidst the poverty, there was love. My earliest memories are of a home filled with music, books, and a deep sense of family, even as we navigated a world that often felt unstable.

THE SEARCH FOR MEANING

As I grew, so did my questions. The conventional path of school and career felt hollow. I saw a world obsessed with material success but spiritually bankrupt. This disconnect fueled a deep restlessness within me. I left the well-trodden path to find my own. This search led me to the practice of Vipassana meditation, a profound turning point that taught me to observe reality as it is, not as I wanted it to be. It was the beginning of deconstructing years of societal and familial conditioning. I embarked on travels across Nepal and India, not as a tourist, but as a seeker. I worked on organic farms, stayed in ashrams, and learned from the land and its people. This period was not about accumulating knowledge, but about shedding the unnecessary to uncover the essential truths of existence, balance, and purpose.

"I had to unlearn everything I was taught to discover what was truly real. My education began the day I left the classroom."

A PATH OF SERVICE

True knowledge demands action. My journey turned from inward discovery to outward service. I found purpose in education, not as a system of rote memorization, but as a tool for liberation. I joined Maya Universe Academy, a free private school, first as a volunteer and eventually as the school principal. Here, I saw firsthand the potential of a different kind of learning—one rooted in community, compassion, and practical skills. This experience solidified my belief that the solutions to our global crises do not lie in new policies or technologies alone, but in a fundamental redesign of how we live, learn, and relate to one another. It was through this service that the scattered pieces of my journey—the rebellion of my parents, the struggles of my youth, the silence of meditation, and the wisdom of the earth—began to coalesce into a coherent vision. This vision would become the Hunate Project.
