My Story

A journey is not a straight line. Mine has been a winding path through darkness and light, a story of losing everything to find a deeper purpose.

The Early Years: Roots and Rupture

Born in 1991 in a remote village in Ilam, Nepal, my life began amidst the simplicity of nature. My family, like many others, migrated to the plains of Jhapa in search of better opportunities. Those early years were shaped by the struggle for stability, a reality that grounds my perspective to this day. However, adolescence brought a profound and dangerous rupture. Searching for identity and belonging, I fell into the grips of drug addiction, a path that led me away from my family and into a cycle of desperation that would define the next chapter of my life.

The Turning Point: A Cell of Contemplation

My addiction was not a private struggle; it led me to crime and, ultimately, to prison. This was my rock bottom. Yet, within the confines of a jail cell, I found an unexpected and profound freedom. Stripped of all external distractions, I was forced to confront myself. This period of forced contemplation became a crucible for transformation. It was in this darkness that I began to question the very nature of the systems that create such desperation. I saw firsthand how society fails its most vulnerable, and a conviction grew within me: there had to be a better way to live, not just for me, but for everyone.

"Prison broke my body, but it freed my mind. It was in losing my liberty that I began to understand the true meaning of freedom."

A New Path: Education and Service

Upon my release, I was a changed man with a new purpose. I immersed myself in service, volunteering and traveling, seeking to understand the world I had been so disconnected from. This path led me to education, where I saw the potential to shape the future. I worked my way up, eventually becoming the principal of a school. In this role, I was not just an administrator; I was a mentor, a guide, and a student of human potential. I saw the immense curiosity and goodness in children, but I also saw how the conventional education system often stifles it. My experiences as an educator became the testing ground for the ideas that would later form the Hunate philosophy.
